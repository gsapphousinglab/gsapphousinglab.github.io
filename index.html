UNTAPPED NYC (BETA VERSION)

Map exploring the potential affordable Additional Dwelling Unit neighborhoods across NYC.

   

New York City has a surplus of unbuilt space, much of it in excellent locations near transit and jobs and with zero acquisition cost and as-of right zoning for a floor or two of multifamily housing. This isn't a dream, but it is on the rooftops. Over half of New York City's multifamily units were built between 1901-1930 in remarkably similar buildings: four to six story walkups with deep back courtyards and fifty-foot frontages. More than three quarters have had no modifications since construction. Over 9,500 of these buildings have 'excess' zoned capacity - meaning they could be built several more stories without any special zoning changes.

The time to build is now. Untapped NYC shows the location of these buildings, their unused floorspace -as of right- and attaches each one to a financial feasibility calculator. UntappedNYC aims to provide a sense of the scale to which New York City -in some of the best locations- can add more housing units. We advocate strongly that these units be affordable, accessible, inclusive, climate-adaptive and targeted to the most vulnerable New Yorkers. This project has been developed in close conversations with practitioners at New York City's Housing Preservation and Development (HPD), design firms, developers and community organizations focusing on affordable housing.

Three physical factors enable a unique moment for feasibility in 2021:

  1. the buildings have a nearly uniform roof footprint and structural capacity, given their construction time and the strict codes followed. For examples of design and line-by-line callouts to the zoning, multifamily dwelling, and building regulations, see our report: (Re)code NYC from December 2020.
  2. advances in modular construction enable additions to be lighter, more resilient, and financially feasible. For some examples -including a downloadable spreadsheet with costs, u-value and materials analysis- the lab's report 'Rooftop Housing for Survival' will debut in September 2021.
  3. an increased appreciation and attention to the operations of successful affordable housing can help power the financing and sustained maintenance of these structures - to make them not just units, but connected to ecosystems of 'home'.

Read more of the Lab's related work on what works and what hasn't in our design/policy/planning briefs: Density and The Role of Cross-Building Networks and Amenities, Where Mixed-Income Housing Works Well, alternate models of maintenance and ownership in Limited-Income, Limited Equity Co-ops and examinations of successful 'non-standard' housing in Single-Room Occupancy (SRO) units.
